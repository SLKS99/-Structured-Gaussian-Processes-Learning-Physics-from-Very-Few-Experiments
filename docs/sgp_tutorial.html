Structured Gaussian Processes: Learning Physics from
Very Few Experiments

By Sheryl L. Sanchez and Yongtao Liu  ·  Based on work with Maxim Ziatdinov's GPax library  ·  August 2023

When exploring a new material system, measurements are expensive and the underlying physics is only partially known. Structured Gaussian Processes (sGP) solve both problems at once: they use physically-motivated functional forms as a prior mean, letting a small number of experiments simultaneously fit the data and recover the governing equations. This tutorial walks through the method step by step — from a controlled toy model to real photoluminescence data on hybrid perovskite thin films — showing exactly how sGP learns physics faster than a black-box GP.

Section 1

Background: From GP to Structured GP

What is a Gaussian Process?

A Gaussian Process (GP) is a probability distribution over functions. Instead of assuming a specific functional form, a GP says: "I believe the true curve is a smooth, correlated function, and I will let the data tell me what it looks like." At every unmeasured point, the GP produces not just a single prediction but a full distribution — a mean estimate surrounded by uncertainty bands that shrink as you add data.

Formally, we model the observations as:

Model

y(x) = f(x) + ε, where f ~ GP(0, k(x, x')) and ε ~ N(0, σ²)

The kernel k(x, x') encodes how correlated two function values are based on distance in input space. Popular choices: RBF (infinite smoothness), Matérn (finite smoothness, more physical).

GPs are powerful — but they are also agnostic. With a zero mean and an RBF kernel, the model has no idea whether the physics is linear, exponential, or piecewise. It must discover everything from data. For expensive experiments (each synthesis run may cost hundreds of dollars and hours of instrument time), this is a serious limitation.

What makes it "structured"?

A Structured GP (sGP) encodes your physical intuition directly into the model by replacing the zero mean function with a parameterized, physically-motivated function:

Key Idea

f ~ GP(μ(x; θ), k(x, x'))

The mean function μ(x; θ) carries your physical hypothesis (e.g., "bandgap varies linearly with composition"). The kernel handles residuals that the physics model doesn't capture. Critically, both the data and the model parameters θ are inferred simultaneously via MCMC — so you recover not just a fit, but posterior distributions over the physical constants themselves.

Conceptual Comparison: GP vs sGP with only 4 observations

With only 4 points, the GP is maximally uncertain between observations. It cannot use physics to interpolate.

With the same 4 points, sGP uses a physical mean function to stay close to the true curve — and simultaneously infers the governing parameters.

Standard GP

  • Mean = 0 (no prior knowledge)
  • High data requirements to reduce uncertainty
  • Cannot extract physical constants
  • Flexible — works on any smooth function

Structured GP (sGP)

  • Mean = physically-motivated function
  • Data-efficient — physics fills in the gaps
  • Posterior samples = distributions over physical constants
  • Interpretable — each parameter has physical meaning

The Physics: Bandgap Engineering in Hybrid Perovskites

Hybrid perovskites and composition tuning

Hybrid organic-inorganic perovskites (ABX₃ structure) are exceptional light-absorbing materials, reaching >25% efficiency in solar cells. Their most powerful feature is compositional tunability: by mixing two end-member perovskites (e.g., MAPbI₃ and GAPbBr₃), you can dial in the optical bandgap across a wide range.

In the MAPbI₃ / GAPbBr₃ system studied here:

  • MAPbI₃ (0% GAPbBr₃): Eg ≈ 1.57 eV — near-ideal for single-junction solar cells
  • GAPbBr₃ (100% GAPbBr₃): Eg ≈ 2.4–2.5 eV — suitable for tandem top cells and LEDs
How Bandgap is Measured

We excite films with a laser and collect the photoluminescence (PL) spectrum. The peak emission wavelength λ_peak corresponds to photons emitted at the bandgap energy:

E_g = hc / λ_peak = 1240 eV·nm / λ_peak[nm]

A plate reader measures many wells simultaneously, giving us PL spectra for tens of compositions in a single experiment.

Vegard's law and optical bowing

The simplest model for how bandgap varies with composition is Vegard's law — a linear interpolation between end members:

Vegard's Law

E_g(x) = (1 − x) · E_g(A) + x · E_g(B)

where x is the mole fraction of component B. Real alloys often deviate from this line — the deviation is called optical bowing:

E_g(x) = (1 − x) · E_g(A) + x · E_g(B) − b · x(1 − x)

The bowing parameter b captures the nonlinearity from structural distortion, orbital hybridization, and disorder.

This gives us a physical hypothesis to embed in sGP: we expect the bandgap to follow a roughly linear or quadratic curve with composition, with a possible kink or bowing. Instead of guessing b, sGP will infer it from the data.

Tutorial Part 1: Toy Model

Before touching real experimental data, we validate the method on a synthetic "toy" system where we know the ground truth exactly. This lets us directly check whether sGP recovers the correct physics — not just a good fit.

Our synthetic bandgap curve is piecewise: quadratic below a transition concentration, then approximately flat above it — mimicking a real alloy where one structural phase dominates at low incorporation and a second at high incorporation.

Step 1: Creating the Toy Dataset

We define a ground-truth function that simulates bandgap vs. composition. Below the transition concentration (c = 1.5), the bandgap follows a quadratic curve. Above it, it flattens out (linear region). We add a tiny amount of noise to mimic measurement uncertainty.

python 
np.random.seed(0)

NUM_POINTS = 200   # total grid points in composition space
NOISE      = 0.01   # small noise (clean experiment)
x_         = np.linspace(0.0, 2.0, NUM_POINTS)   # composition axis

def true_bandgap(x, c=1.5):
    """Ground truth: piecewise — quadratic then flat-ish.
    Transition at c mimics a structural phase boundary."""
    return np.piecewise(
        x, [x < c, x >= c],
        [lambda x: x*x - 1,     # quadratic region (low x)
         lambda x: -x + 2]         # linear region (high x)
    )

y_data = true_bandgap(x_) + np.random.normal(0, NOISE, NUM_POINTS)

plt.scatter(x_, y_data, s=8, alpha=0.6, label="Simulated observations")
plt.xlabel("Composition x"); plt.ylabel("Bandgap Eg (eV)")
plt.title("Ground truth: piecewise quadratic")
Ground truth parameters

In the quadratic regime: a₁ = 1, b₁ = −1. Transition at t = 1.5. In the linear regime: slope a₂ = −1, b₂ = 2. When sGP recovers these, it has genuinely learned the physics.

Step 2: Standard GP as Baseline

We first run a standard (unstructured) GP — RBF kernel, zero mean, noise half-normal prior. This is our baseline. We start with only 2 observations (the two endpoints of the composition range) and let the GP perform active exploration for 30 steps using an uncertainty-driven acquisition function.

python 
# Custom priors on kernel hyperparameters
def kernel_prior():
    k_length = numpyro.sample("k_length", dist.Gamma(0.5, 1))
    k_scale  = numpyro.sample("k_scale",  dist.LogNormal(0, 2))
    return {"k_length": k_length, "k_scale": k_scale}

noise_prior = lambda: numpyro.sample("noise", dist.HalfNormal(0.01))

def step_GP(X_train, y_train, X_test):
    rng_key1, rng_key2 = gpax.utils.get_keys()

    # Initialize standard GP — no mean function
    gp = gpax.ExactGP(1, kernel='RBF',
                      kernel_prior=kernel_prior,
                      noise_prior=noise_prior)

    # Run HMC to sample posterior over kernel hyperparameters
    gp.fit(rng_key1, X_train, y_train, jitter=1e-5)

    # Predict and compute uncertainty-driven acquisition
    y_pred, y_samples = gp.predict_in_batches(rng_key2, X_test,
                                               noiseless=False)
    acq = gpax.acquisition.UE(rng_key2, gp, X_test, noiseless=False)

    return acq, y_pred, y_samples, gp.get_samples()

The Uncertainty Exploration (UE) acquisition function selects the next composition to measure where the GP is most uncertain — efficiently mapping out the bandgap curve.

Step 3: Defining Physics-Informed Mean Functions

Here is where the physics goes in. We define several candidate mean functions, each encoding a different physical hypothesis. For each, we also specify Bayesian priors over the parameters — encoding what we believe before seeing the data.

ModelMean functionHypothesis
GPμ = 0No physics assumed
sGP-linearax + bVegard's law (linear mixing)
sGP-piecewisea₁x+b₁ if x < t, else a₂x+b₂Two-phase linear mixing
sGP-quadraticax² + bx + cQuadratic bowing throughout
c-sGPa₁x²+b₁ if x < t, else a₂+b₂Custom: correct physics model
python — custom sGP mean function 
# ── Piecewise-quadratic mean: matches our ground truth structure ──
def piecewise_quadratic(x: jnp.ndarray, params: Dict) -> jnp.ndarray:
    """Quadratic below the transition, flat above.
    Encodes: two structural phases with different Eg-vs-composition behavior."""
    return jnp.piecewise(
        x,
        [x < params["t"], x >= params["t"]],
        [lambda x: params["a1"] * x**2 + params["b1"],   # low-x: quadratic
         lambda x: params["a2"] + params["b2"]]         # high-x: constant
    )

def piecewise_quadratic_prior():
    """Priors over the physical parameters.
    These encode our expectations BEFORE seeing the data."""
    a1 = numpyro.sample("a1", dist.LogNormal(0, 1))  # quadratic coeff (positive)
    b1 = numpyro.sample("b1", dist.Normal(0, 2))      # vertical shift, low-x
    a2 = numpyro.sample("a2", dist.LogNormal(0, 1))  # amplitude, high-x plateau
    b2 = numpyro.sample("b2", dist.Normal(0, 2))      # shift, high-x plateau
    t  = numpyro.sample("t",  dist.Gamma(0.4, 1))     # transition concentration
    return {"a1": a1, "b1": b1, "a2": a2, "b2": b2, "t": t}
Why LogNormal for a₁?

The quadratic coefficient a₁ must be positive (we expect the curve to curve upward in the low-x phase). LogNormal(0,1) places probability only on positive values while allowing a wide range. Normal(0,2) for offsets because they can be positive or negative.

Step 4: Running the sGP Exploration Loop

We start with just 2 endpoint measurements and run 30 active learning steps. At each step, the sGP (1) fits posterior distributions over all parameters, (2) predicts the full curve, and (3) selects the next composition to measure using the uncertainty acquisition function.

python 
def step_sGP(X_train, y_train, X_test, mean_fn, mean_fn_prior):
    """Single sGP step: fit → predict → acquire next point."""
    rng_key1, rng_key2 = gpax.utils.get_keys()

    # Initialize structured GP with your physics mean function
    sgp = gpax.ExactGP(
        1, kernel='RBF',
        kernel_prior=None,          # use default priors on kernel params
        mean_fn=mean_fn,
        mean_fn_prior=mean_fn_prior,
        noise_prior=noise_prior
    )

    # MCMC (HMC) samples ALL parameters jointly:
    # kernel hyperparameters + physics mean function parameters
    sgp.fit(rng_key1, X_train, y_train, jitter=1e-4)

    # Predict on the unmeasured grid
    y_pred, y_samples = sgp.predict_in_batches(
        rng_key2, X_test, noiseless=True, jitter=1e-4
    )

    # Uncertainty-driven acquisition: go where the model is least sure
    acq = gpax.acquisition.UE(rng_key2, sgp, X_test,
                               noiseless=False, jitter=1e-4)

    return acq, y_pred, y_samples, sgp.get_samples()

# ── Active exploration loop ──
exploration_steps = 30
X = jnp.linspace(0.0, 2.0, NUM_POINTS)
X_train = jnp.asarray([X[0], X[-1]])      # start with only endpoints
y_train = jnp.asarray([y_data[0], y_data[-1]])

all_a1, all_b1, all_t = [], [], []      # track parameter evolution

for step in range(exploration_steps):
    acq, y_pred, y_samples, params = step_sGP(
        X_train, y_train, X_test,
        piecewise_quadratic, piecewise_quadratic_prior
    )
    # Record inferred physics parameters at this step
    all_a1.append(params['a1'].mean())
    all_b1.append(params['b1'].mean())
    all_t.append(params['t'].mean())

What the results tell us

c-sGP reaches lower MSE faster than standard GP — it needs fewer measurements to achieve the same accuracy. More importantly, by ~step 15 the inferred transition t has converged to the true value of 1.5, and a₁ converges to 1.0. The model has not just fit the data — it has discovered the phase boundary and the curvature of the Eg-vs-x curve.

The wrong mean functions (linear, standard GP) plateau at higher MSE because they cannot represent the piecewise-quadratic structure. This demonstrates a key principle: the more your mean function matches the true physics, the more efficiently sGP learns.

Now we apply the same approach to actual experimental photoluminescence data collected on a plate reader from MAPbI₃ / GAPbBr₃ thin films across a range of compositions.

Step 1: Loading the Photoluminescence Data

The plate reader outputs a CSV with PL emission spectra (500–850 nm, 1 nm step) for each well on a 96-well plate. Each well contains a thin film with a specific GAPbBr₃ incorporation percentage. We parse this into a dictionary of spectra and a table of compositions.

Step 2: Extracting Bandgap Energies from Peak Positions

For each spectrum, we find the wavelength of peak emission and convert it to an energy using the photon energy relation. This gives us our dataset: composition (% GAPbBr₃) versus bandgap (eV).

python 
compositions_all = []
bandgaps_all     = []

for i, (well, spectrum) in enumerate(spectra.items()):
    # Find the peak emission wavelength (argmax of PL spectrum)
    peak_idx        = np.argmax(spectrum)
    peak_wavelength = wavelengths[peak_idx]

    # Convert to bandgap energy: E_g = hc / λ = 1240 eV·nm / λ[nm]
    # This is the standard approximation (h = 4.136e-15 eV·s, c = 3e8 m/s)
    E_g = 1240.0 / peak_wavelength

    compositions_all.append(composition[well].values[0])   # % GAPbBr₃
    bandgaps_all.append(E_g)

compositions_all = np.array(compositions_all)   # shape (N,)
bandgaps_all     = np.array(bandgaps_all)        # shape (N,) in eV

# Save for sGP input
data = np.column_stack([compositions_all, bandgaps_all])
np.savetxt('bandgap_vs_composition.csv', data,
           delimiter=',', header='composition_%GAPbBr3,bandgap_eV')

plt.scatter(compositions_all, bandgaps_all, c='#1a2844', s=35, alpha=0.8)
plt.xlabel('GAPbBr₃ incorporation (%)')
plt.ylabel('Bandgap E_g (eV)')
plt.title('Extracted bandgaps across compositional series')
What to look for

Plot your bandgap vs. composition scatter and look for: monotonic increase (expected from Vegard's law), any kinks (possible phase transitions), and scatter at intermediate compositions (inhomogeneous mixing, disorder). These guide your choice of mean function.

Step 3: Fitting sGP to Experimental Data

For MAPbI₃/GAPbBr₃, the bandgap-vs-composition plot shows a roughly linear trend (consistent with Vegard's law). We start with a linear mean function and later compare with piecewise and quadratic options.

python 
# ── Linear mean: E_g(x) ≈ b + a·x  (Vegard's law approximation) ──
def linear(x, params):
    """Linear Vegard-type mean function.
    a = Eg slope (eV per % GAPbBr3)
    b = endpoint Eg at 0% GAPbBr3 (pure MAPbI3)"""
    return params["a"] * x + params["b"]

def linear_prior():
    a = numpyro.sample("a", dist.LogNormal(0, 1))    # slope must be positive
    b = numpyro.sample("b", dist.Uniform(1, 3))      # Eg of MAPbI3 ~ 1.5–1.7 eV
    return {"a": a, "b": b}

noise_prior = lambda: numpyro.sample("noise", dist.HalfNormal(0.01))

# ── Start from endpoint compositions only ──
X_exp = np.array([0.0, 98.0])     # 0% and ~98% GAPbBr3
y_exp = np.array([1.575, 2.430])  # measured bandgaps at endpoints (eV)

# ── Fit sGP ──
rng_key, rng_key_pred = gpax.utils.get_keys()

sgp = gpax.ExactGP(
    1, kernel='Matern',             # Matern kernel: more physical (finite smoothness)
    mean_fn=linear,
    mean_fn_prior=linear_prior,
    kernel_prior=None,
    noise_prior=noise_prior
)
sgp.fit(rng_key, X_exp, y_exp, jitter=1e-4)

# ── Predict over full composition range ──
X_pred  = jnp.linspace(0, 98, 200)[:, None]
y_mean, y_samples = sgp.predict_in_batches(rng_key_pred, X_pred,
                                            noiseless=True, jitter=1e-4)

# ── Acquisition: next composition to measure ──
acq = gpax.acquisition.UE(rng_key_pred, sgp, X_pred,
                           noiseless=False, jitter=1e-4)
next_composition = X_pred[acq.argmax()]
print(f"Next recommended composition: {float(next_composition):.1f}% GAPbBr₃")

As you add more measured compositions (the plate reader measures many at once — just drop newly confirmed wells into the training set), run the loop again. Each iteration tightens the posterior over a and b.

Step 4: Reading the Physics from Posterior Parameters

The real payoff of sGP is in the posterior parameter distributions — not just the curve fit. After training, call sgp.get_samples() to retrieve MCMC samples for every parameter.

python 
params = sgp.get_samples()   # dict of arrays, shape (n_chains × n_samples,)

# ── Posterior means and 95% credible intervals ──
for name, samples in params.items():
    lo, hi = np.percentile(samples, [2.5, 97.5])
    print(f"{name:10s}  mean={samples.mean():.4f}   95% CI: [{lo:.4f}, {hi:.4f}]")

# ── Plot posterior distributions ──
fig, axes = plt.subplots(1, 2, figsize=(10, 4))

axes[0].hist(params['a'], bins=40, color='#0d8c7a', alpha=0.8)
axes[0].axvline(params['a'].mean(), c='#d97b0e', lw=2)
axes[0].set_xlabel('Slope a (eV per % GAPbBr₃)')
axes[0].set_title('Posterior: Vegard slope')

axes[1].hist(params['b'], bins=40, color='#1a2844', alpha=0.8)
axes[1].axvline(params['b'].mean(), c='#d97b0e', lw=2)
axes[1].set_xlabel('Intercept b (Eg at 0% GAPbBr₃, eV)')
axes[1].set_title('Posterior: MAPbI₃ bandgap estimate')
Interpreting the numbers

Slope a: the rate at which the bandgap increases per percent GAPbBr₃ incorporation. A value of ~0.009 eV/% is consistent with the known ~0.87 eV total tuning range over 0–98%.

Intercept b: the estimated bandgap of pure MAPbI₃, inferred from the entire compositional series. This should agree with your directly-measured endpoint value (~1.57 eV). If it doesn't, it may indicate structural inhomogeneity or a miscalibrated plate reader at low concentrations.

Narrow posteriors mean the data is consistent with your physical model. Wide posteriors mean either too little data or the model is misspecified — try switching to a piecewise or quadratic mean.

Which mean function fits best?

Compare models by running each with the same data and tracking total predictive uncertainty (lower = more confident, better constrained by the physics prior):

python 
models = {
    'GP (no prior)':   (None,              None),
    'sGP linear':      (linear,             linear_prior),
    'sGP piecewise':   (piecewise,          piecewise_prior),
    'sGP quadratic':   (quadratic,          quadratic_prior),
}

results = {}
for name, (fn, prior) in models.items():
    _, y_pred, y_samples, _ = step_sGP(X_exp, y_exp, X_pred, fn, prior) \
                              if fn else step_GP(X_exp, y_exp, X_pred)
    total_unc = y_samples.std(axis=(0,1)).sum()
    results[name] = total_unc
    print(f"{name:25s}  total uncertainty = {total_unc:.4f}")
In the MAPbI₃/GAPbBr₃ system

The linear sGP performs best early in exploration — confirming that Vegard-like linear mixing is a good first approximation for this system. As more data is added, the piecewise model may become preferred if the data reveals a kink near certain compositions (potentially a miscibility gap or ordering transition).

Key Takeaways

01

Embed physics as a mean function

The mean function is your hypothesis. Start with the simplest physical model (linear Vegard) and add complexity (piecewise, bowing term) only as the data demands it.

02

You get parameters, not just predictions

MCMC gives you full posterior distributions over every physical constant — slopes, transition points, bandgap offsets. The uncertainty in those parameters tells you what the data hasn't yet resolved.

03

Active learning dramatically cuts data needs

Starting from just two endpoint measurements, the uncertainty-driven acquisition function navigates you to the compositions that most efficiently constrain the physics — often 5–10× fewer experiments than random sampling.

04

Model selection is physical reasoning

Comparing GP vs. sGP-linear vs. c-sGP isn't just statistics — it's asking "which physical mechanism best explains this material?" When c-sGP wins, you've identified a phase transition.
